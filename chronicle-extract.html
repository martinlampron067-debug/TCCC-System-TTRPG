The White Hunger

The wind didn’t just blow in the Saguenay region backwoods; it screamed[cite: 2168]. Odilon Thorne spat a dark stream of tobacco into the snow, watching it freeze before it even sank[cite: 2169]. Beside him, his partner, Kaelig, was tightening the cinches on their last pack horse[cite: 2170]. Bound in rusted chains in the sled behind them sat Clement Tremblay—the man worth one hundred dollars in Chicoutimi[cite: 2172]. "The spirits of the air are turning sharp," the village Elder warned them. "If you go now, you fight the Witiko"[cite: 2175, 2176]. By the third day, the world turned white[cite: 2181]. The horses’ ribs began to show, and the silence of the northern woods was a waiting one[cite: 2181]. On the seventh day, the last horse collapsed, and they had no choice but to slaughter it[cite: 2187, 2188]. As they ate, the atmosphere shifted. The temperature plummeted until the trees began to crack like pistol shots[cite: 2189]. Odilon looked at Kaelig. His partner’s eyes were bloodshot, staring at the horse meat with a terrifying, ravenous intensity[cite: 2190]. "There's not enough," Kaelig whispered[cite: 2191]. A blur of impossible darkness tore through the camp[cite: 2200]. As it passed, a spindly, translucent hand brushed against the prisoner’s cheek[cite: 2206]. Tremblay didn't scream. He gasped, his back arching, and a line of black frost began to spread where the shadow had touched his skin[cite: 2207, 2217]. The trees began to groan his name: “Clé-ment... Clé-ment Trem-blay...”[cite: 2213, 2214]. Days later, Kaelig’s madness broke. He lunged at Tremblay, sinkings his teeth into the prisoner’s cheek[cite: 2232, 2255]. A guttural scream tore from Kaelig’s throat as Tremblay tore a jagged piece of flesh away[cite: 2256]. The blood wasn't red—it was oily black ichor[cite: 2258]. Odilon stood alone in the dark after the final shots were fired[cite: 2310]. He was alone, without food, dragging two dead men through a winter that had only just begun[cite: 2314]. By the time the church spires of Chicoutimi pierced the horizon, Odilon was a skeletal remains of a man[cite: 2320]. He walked into the police office like a man stepping out of a tomb[cite: 2322]. "Take your money, man," the Sheriff whispered, wincing at Odilon’s touch. "You look like you're starving"[cite: 2328, 2329, 2332]. Odilon’s eyes, once brown, were now a piercing, translucent blue—the color of a glacier[cite: 2326]. "The money is cold," Odilon whispered. "I’m hungry"[cite: 2331, 2333].
